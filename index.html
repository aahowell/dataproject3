The world's banana problem: 5 questions you were too embarrassed to ask

Updated by Aleah Howell on March 28, 2016

What banana problem?

Banana plantations are being hit by a fungal disease called Tropical Race 4, a strain of Panama disease. With no banana resistant to the strain, the United Nations says the disease threatens global supply of one of the world's top food crops. Top banana exporters, Ecuador, Philippines and other Latin American countries have yet to be hit by the disease and are taking steps to limit the risk. However, while prevention measures will stall the arrival of Race 4, scientists are saying it is inevitable that the disease spreads to all producing countries.

Where do my bananas come from?

15 percent of the world's bananas are grown primarily for export. The majority of the United States and Europe's bananas come from Latin America. Many top producing countries grow bananas mainly for domestic consumption.

Do we eat that many bananas?

Americans eat more bananas than any other fruit, with an average consumption of 26 pounds per year. Americans ate an average of 117.2 pounds of fresh and processed fruit per person in 2013. Bananas and apples top the list of most popular fresh fruits followed by fresh oranges.

Are my bananas more expensive now?

The price of the banana is surprisingly cheap. However, the era of the cheap banana might not last forever. The standardization of the Cavendish banana has allowed agribusinesses to grow bananas for low cost. Tropical Race 4 is very unlikely to wipe out the banana entirely. But it could devastate banana crops and drive up retail prices of the fruit in coming years.
